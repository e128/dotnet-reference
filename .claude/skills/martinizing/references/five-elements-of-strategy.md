# Roger Martin's Strategy Framework

Roger L. Martin's strategy framework, developed over three decades and published in
*Playing to Win: How Strategy Really Works* (2013, co-authored with A.G. Lafley). The framework
has two distinct components: a **definition of strategy** and the **Strategy Choice Cascade** —
a companion tool for creating strategy.

For the full cascade question breakdowns, product/tech application table, common mistakes,
and why the definition changed, see [references/cascade.md](references/cascade.md).

## What Strategy Is NOT

Martin identifies three common misuses of the word "strategy" that produce no strategic value:

| Misuse | Example | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| A list of initiatives | A "strategic plan" with funded projects | A list is not a set of integrated choices |
| An adjective for importance | "Strategic sourcing," "strategic HR" | A modifier of another word, not a strategy |
| A dream | "Our strategy is to become the best X" | Hope is not a strategy |

## The Definition of Strategy

### Original Definition (mid-1990s) — Five Elements

Martin's original definition: **"Strategy is an integrated set of choices that uniquely positions
the firm in its industry so as to create sustainable advantage and superior value relative to the
competition."**

Five elements: Choices, Integrated set, Positions the firm (Where-to-Play), Creates sustainable
advantage (How-to-Win), Superior financial returns.

Martin used this definition for a quarter-century before revising it.

### Revised Definition (2022) — Three Elements

Martin's tighter definition: **"Strategy is an integrated set of choices that compel desired
customer action."**

#### 1. Choices (kept)

Strategy is made of choices — to do some things and not others.

**The "Not Stupid on Its Face" Test:** A strategy choice is one for which the opposite of the
choice is *not stupid on its face*.

- "Be customer-centric" **fails** this test — the opposite (ignoring customers) IS stupid on its face
- Such choices are **operating imperatives** — important, even critical, but not strategic
- A genuine strategic choice is one where a reasonable competitor might choose the opposite

#### 2. Integrated Set (kept)

The choices must fit together and reinforce one another — not a disconnected list. It is an
integrated set if the choices across the five cascade questions fit with and reinforce each other.

#### 3. Desired Customer Action (NEW)

The central insight: **a company does not control the customer.**

A company controls many things — employees, factories, advertising spend, R&D investment,
distribution channels. The one essential element over which the company has zero control is the
**customer**. Therefore, the task of strategy is to make choices in areas under the company's
control that together compel desired customer action.

| Outcome | Result |
|---|---|
| Many buy, but only at too low a price | Failed strategy |
| Customers buy at target price, but too few | Failed strategy |
| Enough customers buy at sufficient prices | Strategy is working |

Consistent with Peter Drucker's view: the purpose of a business is to create and keep a customer.

## The Strategy Choice Cascade — Overview

Five interconnected questions, ordered most abstract to most concrete:

1. **What Is Our Winning Aspiration?** — Define what winning means; playing to win, not to participate
2. **Where Will We Play?** — Choose the arena (geography, customers, products, channels, value chain)
3. **How Will We Win?** — Cost leadership or differentiation in the chosen arena
4. **What Capabilities Must Be in Place?** — A reinforcing system, not a laundry list
5. **What Management Systems Are Required?** — Metrics, incentives, structure (most neglected level)

**Toggle, don't sequence** — iterate back and forth; it is not one-way top-down.
